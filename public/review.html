Treatment of JAK2-Negative / Idiopathic Erythrocytosis

last appended 2026/05/19

Running literature review. Compiled 2026/05/19.

TL;DR

There are no RCTs in idiopathic erythrocytosis (IE). Every recommendation is extrapolated from polycythemia vera (CYTO-PV) or based on expert consensus and retrospective audits. The current weight of evidence supports a conservative, individualized approach: avoid cytoreduction, do not phlebotomize asymptomatic patients to “normal” Hct, target CV risk factor optimization, and reserve phlebotomy + low-dose aspirin for patients with hyperviscosity symptoms, prior thrombosis, or significant CV risk.

1. The evidence base — why it’s thin

  • No randomized trial has ever been done in IE. The Tefferi 2025 ASH review and the 2026 Mayo update both state explicitly there are no evidence-based guidelines.
  • CYTO-PV (Marchioli, NEJM 2013) — the only RCT of Hct target in any erythrocytosis — randomized 365 PV patients to Hct <45% vs 45–50%. Tight control reduced CV death + major thrombosis by ~4× (HR ~3.9). This is the foundation of the Hct <45% target in PV. It does not apply to IE: in CYTO-PV every patient had JAK2-mutated clonal disease, leukocytosis, splenomegaly, and a thrombotic biology that IE patients do not share.
  • Curtin et al. 2025 (dual-centre 5-yr retrospective, n=206 JAK2-negative secondary/idiopathic erythrocytosis): venesection conferred no reduction in thrombosis or mortality (HR 0.88, ns). Mean Hct fell ~0.04–0.05 over 5 yr regardless of phlebotomy. Thrombosis rates: VTE 2.9%, arterial 7.3% over median 6.2 yr — broadly comparable to age-matched general population.
  • Mayo 2020 (Gangat) and 2021 phenotypic comparison studies: secondary erythrocytosis has thrombosis rates comparable to PV at diagnosis in some series, but the driver appears to be the underlying CV risk profile (smoking, OSA, hypoxia, androgens), not the Hct itself.

The repeated message across the 2021, 2023, 2025, and 2026 Tefferi/Szuber syntheses: “there is no substantive evidence that an increase in Hgb/Hct per se drives thrombosis” in JAK2-unmutated disease.

2. Hematocrit threshold and target — what guidelines actually say

Population Threshold to act Target Hct Grade
Idiopathic erythrocytosis (BSH 2019) Symptomatic hyperviscosity OR prior thrombosis OR high CV risk <0.55 generally; <0.45 if prior thrombosis 2C
Hypoxic pulmonary disease (BSH 2019) Hct >0.56 with hyperviscosity symptoms 0.50–0.52 (lower is detrimental) 2C; LTOT 1A
Post-renal transplant (BSH 2019) First line is ACEi/ARB, not phlebotomy If venesecting, <0.50 2B for ACEi, 2C for venesection
Congenital erythrocytosis (BSH 2019) Symptoms or prior thrombosis <0.52 (do not normalize) 2C
Polycythemia vera (CYTO-PV; for contrast) All patients <0.45 1B (RCT)

Tefferi/Szuber 2025 and 2026 reject any fixed Hct threshold in IE — they recommend “a Hct level that provides relief” of hyperviscosity symptoms, with frequency of phlebotomy driven by symptoms, not numbers.

Key practical points: - The 0.45 PV target is not generalizable. Driving Hct to <0.45 in IE has no evidence behind it and in secondary erythrocytosis (especially hypoxic) is harmful. - “Idiopathic” Hct 0.45–0.55 is observation territory unless symptomatic or other risk factors push you to act. - Avoid iatrogenic iron deficiency from repeated venesection — it causes its own symptoms (fatigue, cognitive fog, pica) that get mistaken for hyperviscosity, prompting more phlebotomy, etc.

3. Efficacy of phlebotomy in IE — what’s actually shown

  • Symptomatic improvement: real but anecdotal — headache, plethora, hyperviscosity respond. No controlled data; effect size unknown.
  • Thrombosis reduction: not demonstrated in IE or secondary erythrocytosis (Curtin 2025). The CYTO-PV benefit has never been reproduced outside the JAK2-positive context.
  • Mortality: no signal in either direction.
  • Harms: iron deficiency (very common, often unrecognized), reactive thrombocytosis (rarely clinically significant), vasovagal events, time/cost burden, and in elderly patients with cardiovascular disease the volume shift is not trivial.

Bottom line on phlebotomy: it is a symptom-control intervention in IE, not a thrombosis-prevention one. Frame the discussion with the patient that way.

4. Aspirin — rationale and evidence

The aspirin question splits cleanly:

Where the evidence actually lives: - ECLAP (Landolfi, NEJM 2004) — RCT of low-dose ASA in PV, ~50% relative reduction in composite CV endpoint. PV only. - No RCT in IE or secondary erythrocytosis.

Rationale for offering ASA in IE: - IE patients often carry standard CV risk factors (smoking, dyslipidemia, OSA, sedentary, obesity). - Some series suggest a CV risk profile slightly higher than the general population — possibly because the erythrocytosis itself is a marker of unrecognized cardiovascular/metabolic dysfunction (OSA, fatty liver, androgen use), not because of viscosity. - Low-dose ASA (75–100 mg) has a modest absolute thrombotic benefit and a known bleeding cost; the calculus is the same as in any non-MPN patient.

Recommendations across guidelines: - BSH 2019 (IE): “Aspirin if otherwise clinically indicated for primary or secondary prevention” — Grade 1B. In other words, treat aspirin like you would in any matched non-erythrocytotic adult — don’t add it because of the erythrocytosis, but don’t withhold it either. - Tefferi 2025/2026: ASA “may be considered” for hyperviscosity symptoms, prior thrombosis, or CV comorbidity. - No guideline recommends ASA in asymptomatic IE without CV indications.

Where ASA is NOT recommended: - Post-transplant erythrocytosis: BSH says no benefit (Grade 2C). - Pure asymptomatic IE without CV risk factors: no rationale. - Congenital erythrocytosis: only “consider” (2C) — but several conditions (e.g., high-affinity Hb) have very low thrombosis rates and ASA carries net harm in young patients with decades of exposure.

5. Cytoreduction (HU, IFN, ruxolitinib)

  • Not recommended in IE — consistent across BSH 2019, Tefferi 2021/2023/2025/2026 (Grade 1B against, BSH).
  • One exception: ruxolitinib in Chuvash polycythemia (VHL R200W) — “consider” (BSH 2C), based on a small case series. Unlikely relevant in Taiwan.
  • HIF2A inhibitors (e.g., belzutifan) are being explored for HIF2A gain-of-function congenital erythrocytosis; not standard of care.

6. CV risk optimization — the only universally agreed pillar

Every guideline and every review converges on this as the most important intervention:

  • Smoking cessation (often the single biggest lever).
  • BP control, statin if indicated.
  • OSA screening/treatment.
  • Weight, glycemic control, fatty liver workup.
  • Discontinue exogenous androgens if applicable.
  • Avoid SGLT2i if the erythrocytosis is plausibly drug-induced (now a well-recognized cause — Gangat 2023).

These interventions have direct evidence (general cardiology literature) and address the most likely true thrombotic driver in IE.

7. Applying this to your patient

49F, Hct 49%, JAK2 V617F + exon 12 negative, EPO normal, no obvious secondary cause, no CV risk factors, no prior thrombosis, no symptoms mentioned.

By BSH 2019 + Tefferi 2025 logic: - Hct 0.49 is below the 0.55 action threshold for asymptomatic IE. - No prior thrombosis → no indication to target <0.45. - No CV indication → ASA not indicated solely on the basis of erythrocytosis. - No role for cytoreduction.

Reasonable plan once the missing workup (P50, COHb, overnight oximetry, contrast imaging) is complete and negative: 1. Label as idiopathic erythrocytosis with caveats (no NGS for congenital genes available). 2. Observation with annual CBC and CV risk assessment. 3. Phlebotomy only if hyperviscosity symptoms develop or Hct climbs above ~0.55; in that case target symptom relief, not a number, and protect against iron deficiency. 4. ASA only if independent CV risk profile justifies it (current 10-yr ASCVD calculation), not for the erythrocytosis itself. 5. Re-check JAK2 (consider higher-sensitivity assay if available) every 2–3 years or if phenotype evolves (rising WBC, platelet count, splenomegaly, pruritus). 6. Family history — if any AD pattern emerges, send out for hereditary erythrocytosis panel even if in-house unavailable.
